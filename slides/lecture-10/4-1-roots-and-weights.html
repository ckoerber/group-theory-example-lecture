IV The highest-weight construction scheme

IV.1 Roots and weights

Weights

  • Cartan generators are the maximal set $C \sub A$ of linearly independent generators which commute

    $$\left[\hat{H}_{i}, \hat{H}_{j}\right]=0, \quad \forall \hat{H}_{i}, \hat{H}_{j} \in C \subset A$$

  • A nilpontent algebra $C$ is a set of generators which envetually terminates under commutation

    $\small \left[\hat H_{1},\left[\hat H_{2},\left[\cdots\left[\hat H_{n-1}, \hat H_{n}\right] \right]\right]\right. = 0$ for $\hat H_n \in C$

  • A self-normalizng subalgebra $C \sub A$ is a set of generators which fulfill

    If $[\hat H_i, \hat X] \in C$ for all $\hat H_i \in C$ then also $\hat X \in C$

  • Note that:

    • Linear independence is technically only defined once we introduce a representation; This choice is still unambiguous; for different choices you still get the same result.
    • More mathematically: Cartan subalgebras are nilpotent subalgebras that are self-normalising.
    • The Cartan subalgebra for $su(2)$ is $C = \{ \hat J_3 \}$ (has rank 1).

  • Because the Cartan generators form a subalgebra, one can find a normalized basis
    Find a similarity transformation such that $\hat H_i \to S^{-1}\hat H_i S $ are properly normalized in the Killing form

    $$\operatorname{tr}\left[ D^{(\mathrm{adj})\dagger}\left(\hat H_{i}\right) D^{(\mathrm{adj})}\left(\hat H_{j}\right) \right]=k_{\mathrm{adj}} \delta_{i j}, \quad \forall i, j=1, \cdots, m$$

  • After applying this similarity transformation, we use the eigenvalues of the Cartan generators to label our basis

    $\hat{H}_{i}|D; \vec \mu \rangle = \mu_i |D; \vec \mu \rangle $ for $i=1, \cdots , m$

    • The eigenvalues are called weights (that’s where the name highest-weight scheme is coming from).
    • For $su(N)$, they are all real because the generators are Hermitian.
    • The vector $\vec \mu \equiv \mu$ with components $\mu_i$ is called weight vector.

Revisiting the adjoint representation

  • Structure constants define the commutation relations of an algebra

    $$\left[\hat{X}_{a}, \hat{X}_{b}\right]=i f_{a b c} \hat{X}_{c}$$

  • The adjoint representation is defined by the set of matrices corresponding to structure constatns

    $\left[D^{(\mathrm{adj})}\left(\hat X_{a}\right)\right]_{b c}=-i f_{a b c} \equiv \left[X_{a}^{(\mathrm{adj})}\right]_{bc}$

  • States of the adjoint representation can be directly associated with generators: $ \hat X_a \leftrightarrow |\mathrm{adj}; \hat X_a \rangle$ by

    $$ \small \begin{aligned} \left \langle \mathrm{adj}; \hat X_b \middle| \hat X_a \middle| \mathrm{adj}; \hat X_c \right\rangle &= \left[D^{(\mathrm{adj})}\left(\hat X_{a}\right)\right]_{b c} \\ \left \langle \mathrm{adj}; \hat X_a \middle| \mathrm{adj}; \hat X_b \right\rangle &= \frac{1}{k_{\mathrm{adj}}} \mathrm{tr}\left[ D^{(\mathrm{adj}) \dagger}\left(\hat X_{a}\right) D^{(\mathrm{adj})}\left(\hat X_{b}\right) \right]=\delta_{b a} \end{aligned} $$

  • Because representations are linear, this allows to associate algebra computations (LHS) with state-space computations (RHS)

    $ \left|\mathrm{adj}; \alpha \hat X_a + \beta \hat X_b \right\rangle = \alpha \left|\mathrm{adj}; \hat X_a \right\rangle + \beta \left|\mathrm{adj}; \hat X_b \right\rangle $ for $\hat X_{a}, \hat X_b \in A$ and $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb K$

  • $\Rightarrow$ The action of a genrator on a state corresponds to computing the commutator in the algebra space

    $$ \hat{X}_{a} \left|\mathrm{adj}; \hat X_b \right\rangle = \left|\mathrm{adj}; [\hat X_a, \hat X_b] \right\rangle $$

Roots

  • Roots are the weights (eigenvalues of the Cartan generators) of the adjoint representation

    $\small\hat{H}_{i} \left|\mathrm{adj}; \hat E_{\alpha}\right\rangle = \alpha_{i}\left|\mathrm{adj}; \hat E_{\alpha}\right\rangle$ with $\small \hat E_{\alpha} \in A$ for all $\small i = 1, \cdots m$

    Note that:

    • An algebra can have more than one root.
    • Roots have specific values uniquely determined by the algebra.

  • Superpositions of Cartan generator states correspond to state with zero root vectors

    $ \small \hat H_j \left| \mathrm{adj}; \sum_i c_i \hat H_i \right\rangle = \sum_i c_i \left| \mathrm{adj}; [\hat H_j, \hat H_i] \right\rangle = 0 $ $\forall \hat H_j \in C$

    • Note that, (after choosing an approriate normalization constant for the Killing form) Cartan generator states are orthnormal

  • Root states are orthogonal
    Assuming $\hat E_{\alpha}, \hat E_\beta \neq c_i \hat H_i$ — or equivalently $\alpha, \beta \neq \vec 0$

    $ \left\langle \mathrm{adj}; \hat E_\alpha \mid \mathrm{adj}; \hat E_\beta \right\rangle \sim \prod\limits_{i=1}^{m} \delta_{\alpha_i \beta_i} $

    I.e., this scalar product is only non-zero, if evaluated for the same roots.

Proof

Raising and lowering operators

$su(2)$ ladder operator algebra

$$\begin{aligned} \left[\hat{J}_{3}, \hat{J}^{\pm}\right]&=\pm J^{\pm} \,,& \hat{J}^{+\dagger} &=\hat{J}^{-} \,,& \left[\hat{J}^{+}, \hat{J}^{-}\right]&=\hat{J}_{3} \end{aligned} $$

  • Root generators are "eigenvectors" of Cartan generators under commutation
    And roots are the corresponding "eigenvalues" (this could also be viewed as an alternative definition for roots)

    $ \left[\hat{H}_{i}, \hat{E}_{\alpha}\right]=\alpha_{i} \hat{E}_{\alpha} $ for all $i = 1, \cdots , m$

    Note that this is a statement about the algebra, not the adjoint representation (but, of course, they have a one-to-one correspondance)

  • Generators corresponding to roots are not Hermitian
    Their conjugate changes the sign of the root.

    $\left[\hat{H}_{i}, \hat{E}_{\alpha}^{\dagger}\right]=-\alpha_{i} \hat{E}_{\alpha}^{\dagger}$ and thus $\hat{E}_{+\alpha}^{\dagger}=\hat{E}_{-\alpha}$

  • Root generators change weights (for any, not just the adjoint, representation)

    $$\hat{E}_{\pm \alpha}|D; \mu\rangle \sim| D; \mu \pm \alpha,\rangle$$

    Note that the entire weight vector is changed; some components might be raised, some might be lowered.

  • The commutator of a raising and lowering operator is in the Cartan subalgebra (homework)

    $$\left[\hat{E}_{+\alpha}, \hat{E}_{-\alpha}\right]=\alpha_{i} \hat{H}_{i} \in C$$

Raising and lowering

Ladder operator algebra

$$\begin{aligned} \small \left[\hat{J}_{3}, \hat{J}^{\pm}\right]&=\pm J^{\pm} \,,& \hat{J}^{+\dagger} &=\hat{J}^{-} \,,& \left[\hat{J}^{+}, \hat{J}^{-}\right]&=\hat{J}_{3} \\ \left[\hat{H}_{i}, \hat{E}_{\alpha}\right]&=\alpha_{i} \hat{E}_{\alpha} \,,& \hat{E}_{+\alpha}^{\dagger}&=\hat{E}_{-\alpha} \,,& \left[\hat{E}_{+\alpha}, \hat{E}_{-\alpha}\right]&=\alpha_{i} \hat{H}_{i} \end{aligned} $$

  • Root vectors $\alpha$ correspond to unique generators $\hat E_\alpha$ (modulo normalization)

    • In other words, for a given $\alpha$, we do not find two linearly independent generators $\hat E_\alpha$ and $\hat E_\alpha'$￼ such that

      $[ \hat H_i , \hat E_\alpha ] = \alpha_i \hat E_\alpha$ and $[ \hat H_i , \hat E_\alpha' ] = \alpha_i \hat E_\alpha' \quad \forall i=1, \cdots m $ but $\hat E_\alpha \neq z \hat E_\alpha' $ for $z \in \mathbb C$

  • If $\alpha$￼is a root, then no non-zero multiple of $\alpha$ (except $-\alpha$) is a root

  • Note that for the adjoint representation derivation, we explicitly normalized states according to the Killing form. Here, we can normalize operators $ \hat{E}_{\pm \alpha}$ by an overall factor to fulfill the above equations.

So far, this looks very similar to what we had for
$su(2)$ — modulo normalization factors and vector additions...

  • For each non-zero pair of root vectors $(+ \alpha, - \alpha)$￼, we can an $su(2) \sub A$ subalgebra

    $$\hat X_{\pm\alpha} \equiv \frac{\hat E_{\pm \alpha}}{|\alpha|} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat X_{3}(\alpha) \equiv \frac{\alpha_i \hat H_i}{|\alpha|^2}$$

    This corresponds to normalizing the generators so that they fulfill the $su(2)$ algebra.
